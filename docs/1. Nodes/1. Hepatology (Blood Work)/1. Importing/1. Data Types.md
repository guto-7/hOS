### Data Types — Bloodwork Node

This document applies the framework defined in [Determining Data Types](../../../0.%20Overview/1.%20Importing%20(Data%20Collection)/1.%20Determining%20Data%20Types.md) to the Bloodwork (Hepatology) node.

---

### Sub-Mediums

Bloodwork has a single diagnostic sub-medium: **laboratory blood panel reports** (venous blood samples analysed by accredited pathology laboratories).

Unlike domains such as radiology — which encompasses X-ray, MRI, CT, ultrasound, and other fundamentally different imaging modalities — blood work produces one type of output: a structured report listing measured biomarker values with reference ranges. The analytical methods vary (immunoassay, spectrophotometry, flow cytometry, etc.) but the consumer-facing output is uniform: a tabular report of marker names, numeric values, units, and reference intervals.

This uniformity is what makes bloodwork a strong candidate for deterministic extraction. The data is already structured at source — it just needs to be parsed, not interpreted from raw signal.

**Why only one sub-medium:** There is no meaningful sub-medium split within blood work. Point-of-care testing (finger-prick devices, home glucose monitors) produces individual values rather than panel reports and is excluded — see [Limitations](2.%20Limitations.md). Genetic/genomic blood tests (e.g., pharmacogenomics panels) are a separate diagnostic domain and do not belong in this node.

### Supported Marker Set

The node recognises **62 canonical markers** defined in `data/markers.json`, organised into the following clinical categories. Each category's inclusion is justified by its prevalence in routine and commonly-ordered pathology panels, and by the existence of established, evidence-based reference ranges that enable deterministic evaluation.

#### Tier 1 — Core Panels (routine pathology, ordered in >90% of standard blood work)

These panels form the foundation of clinical blood work. They are ordered in virtually every general health check, hospital admission, and chronic disease monitoring pathway.

| Category | Markers | Count | Basis for Inclusion |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Complete Blood Count (CBC)** | WBC, RBC, Hb, Hct, MCV, MCH, RDW, PLT | 8 | The single most commonly ordered blood test worldwide. Screens for anaemia, infection, clotting disorders, and haematological malignancies. Reference ranges are standardised internationally [1][2]. |
| **Electrolytes & Renal Function** | Na, K, Cl, HCO3, BUN, Cr, eGFR, Ca, AdjCa, PO4, Mg, UA | 12 | Core components of the Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP) and Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP). Kidney function (eGFR from CKD-EPI equation), electrolyte balance, and mineral homeostasis. eGFR is a calculated marker derived from creatinine, age, and sex [3][4]. |
| **Liver Function Tests (LFTs)** | TP, Alb, Glob, ALP, TBil, GGT, AST, ALT | 8 | Standard hepatic panel included in all CMP orders. Screens for hepatocellular damage, cholestasis, and synthetic function. GGT and AST/ALT are sex-differentiated [1][5]. |
| **Lipid Panel** | TC, HDL, LDL, NonHDL, TG, LDL/HDL Ratio, Chol/HDL Ratio | 7 | Cardiovascular risk assessment. Included in all routine health checks for adults. Tiered classification (desirable/borderline/high) follows ATP III and ESC/EAS guidelines. NonHDL and ratios are calculated from direct measurements [6][7]. |
| **Glucose & Glycaemic Control** | Glucose (Fasting), HbA1c | 2 | Diabetes screening and monitoring. Tiered classification (normal/prediabetes/diabetes) follows ADA and WHO diagnostic criteria [8][9]. |

**Tier 1 total: 37 markers**

#### Tier 2 — Extended Panels (commonly ordered, ~50-70% of routine panels)

Frequently ordered alongside core panels or as targeted follow-up. Reference ranges are well-established and standardised across major laboratories.

| Category | Markers | Count | Basis for Inclusion |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Iron Studies** | Ferritin, Fe, Transferrin, TSAT | 4 | Standard investigation for anaemia (flagged by CBC) and iron overload. Ferritin is age- and sex-differentiated with pregnancy-specific ranges. TSAT is derived from iron and transferrin [1][10]. |
| **Thyroid Function** | TSH, FT4, FT3 | 3 | Most commonly ordered endocrine panel. TSH is the first-line screening test; FT4/FT3 are reflexed when TSH is abnormal. Pregnancy-adjusted ranges are clinically significant [11][12]. |
| **Vitamins & Nutritional** | Vitamin D (25-OH), B12, Folate | 3 | Routinely ordered in general health checks, particularly in populations at risk of deficiency (elderly, vegetarians, limited sun exposure). Vitamin D uses tiered classification (deficiency/insufficiency/sufficient/optimal) [13][14]. |

**Tier 2 total: 10 markers**

#### Tier 3 — Specialised Markers (ordered for specific clinical questions)

Not part of routine panels but ordered when clinical presentation warrants investigation. Included because they appear frequently enough on pathology reports that users will upload them, and because established reference ranges exist for deterministic evaluation.

| Category | Markers | Count | Basis for Inclusion |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Metabolic (extended)** | Fasting Insulin | 1 | Insulin resistance screening. Not routine but increasingly ordered alongside glucose/HbA1c for metabolic syndrome assessment [15]. |
| **Inflammation** | hsCRP | 1 | Cardiovascular risk stratification. AHA/CDC tiered risk classification (low/average/high) [16]. |
| **Androgens** | Total Testosterone, SHBG, Free Testosterone | 3 | Ordered for hypogonadism, PCOS, and androgen excess investigation. Sex-differentiated ranges. Free Testosterone is male-only in current implementation [17]. |
| **Adrenal** | DHEA-S | 1 | Adrenal function screening. Sex- and age-dependent but well-characterised ranges [17]. |
| **Pituitary/Gonadal** | FSH, LH, Oestradiol (E2), Progesterone, Prolactin | 5 | Fertility workup, menstrual irregularity, menopause assessment. Female ranges are cycle-phase-dependent (follicular/midcycle/luteal/postmenopausal), requiring patient context [17][18]. |
| **Growth Axis** | IGF-1, GH | 2 | Growth hormone assessment. IGF-1 is age-stratified with four defined brackets. GH has a single upper-bound reference [17]. |
| **Prostate** | PSA | 1 | Prostate cancer screening (male-only). Age-stratified upper limits following RCPA/AACB guidelines [19]. |
| **Homocysteine** | Homocysteine | 1 | Cardiovascular and B-vitamin metabolism marker. Optimal tier defined separately from standard range [20]. |

**Tier 3 total: 15 markers**

### File Formats

#### Primary Format: PDF (digital, text-layer)

The only accepted input format for v1. Justification is documented in [Importing.md](Importing.md) Section 1.1.

**Why PDF:** 95%+ of pathology reports from major Australian and US laboratories are delivered to patients as digital PDFs with an embedded text layer. This is the dominant consumer-facing format — the report the patient downloads from their lab's patient portal or receives via email.

**Provider variation:** All supported laboratories produce text-layer PDFs, but with lab-specific table layouts, header/footer formats, and section ordering. This is handled at the parsing stage (see [Raw Extraction & Formatting](3.%20Raw%20Extraction%20%26%20Formatting.md)) via lab-specific regex templates. Currently supported laboratories:

| Region | Laboratories |
|---|---|
| Australia | Laverty Pathology, QML Pathology, Sullivan Nicolaides Pathology, Australian Clinical Labs, Dorevitch Pathology, Melbourne Pathology |
| United States | Quest Diagnostics, LabCorp |

Additional laboratory templates are added as users encounter them. The system fails gracefully for unrecognised layouts — extraction is attempted but all markers are scored LOW confidence, requiring explicit user confirmation.

#### Excluded Formats

Documented in [Limitations](2.%20Limitations.md). In summary: scanned/image PDFs, photos, CSV, manual entry, and HL7 FHIR are all excluded from v1 with documented rationale.

### Unit Standardisation

Each marker in `markers.json` defines a **canonical unit** and an array of `unit_conversions` for alternate units encountered across labs.

**Canonical unit system:** SI units are used as the canonical standard, consistent with Australian laboratory practice and RCPA reporting guidelines [1]. US laboratories that report in conventional units (e.g., mg/dL for glucose, ng/dL for testosterone) are handled via conversion factors defined per-marker.

| Marker Example | Canonical (SI) | Conventional | Conversion Factor | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Glucose | mmol/L | mg/dL | x 0.0556 | RCPA [1] |
| Creatinine | umol/L | mg/dL | x 88.4 | RCPA [1] |
| Testosterone | nmol/L | ng/dL | x 0.0347 | Endocrine Society [17] |
| Vitamin D | nmol/L | ng/mL | x 2.5 | Endocrine Society [14] |

Conversion factors are sourced from the same reference guidelines that define the marker's reference ranges. Every conversion must be traceable — see References.

### Reference Range Sources

Reference ranges in `markers.json` use the following stratification where clinically indicated:

- **Sex-differentiated:** Creatinine, Haemoglobin, RBC, Hct, GGT, UA, Ferritin, HDL, Testosterone, SHBG, DHEA-S
- **Age-stratified:** IGF-1, PSA
- **Cycle-phase-dependent:** FSH, LH, Oestradiol, Progesterone (follicular/midcycle/luteal/postmenopausal)
- **Pregnancy-adjusted:** K, HCO3, BUN, Cr, PO4, TP, Alb, Glob, ALP, FT4, FT3, Ferritin, UA
- **Tiered (risk classification):** TC, LDL, TG, HDL, Glucose, HbA1c, Vitamin D, hsCRP, Insulin, Homocysteine

> **ACTION REQUIRED:** Each marker's reference range must be individually verified against the cited source and cross-checked with at least one secondary source. Ranges that cannot be traced to a published guideline must be flagged and resolved before the node can be considered validated. This verification is tracked in `docs/references/bloodwork/range_verification.md` (to be created).
